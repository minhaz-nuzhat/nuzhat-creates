// vibecode with a bigtech product engineer · week-by-week · max 60 sec reels
Rule: build something real every week. Narrate the UX/product thinking as you code. Be funny. Be specific.
WEEK 01
Let's build a terminal UI — what a DevEx PM at Microsoft actually thinks about
Build: A styled CLI prompt interface with command history + error feedback
Affordance — does the user know what to type?
Feedback loops — what happens when they get it wrong?
Error recovery — is the error message actually helpful?
Cognitive load — how many commands until it feels overwhelming?
CONTEXT: "I work in the division that shipped VSCode — used by over 80 million developers. Every character in that terminal was a product decision."
WEEK 02
Let's build a startup dashboard — thinking like an Azure Managed Grafana PM
Build: A metrics dashboard — requests/sec, error rate, latency p99
Information hierarchy — what does your eye hit first?
Data-ink ratio — every pixel that isn't data is noise
Progressive disclosure — hide the details until they're needed
Colour as signal — red/amber/green is a language not a decoration
CONTEXT: "I shipped observability features for Azure. Every data decision in a dashboard is a product decision about what your users panic about."
WEEK 03
Let's build a feature flag system — how Microsoft ships safely to millions
Build: Feature toggle UI — enable/disable per user segment, percentage rollout
Experimentation design — why you test before you ship
Traffic splitting UX — what "5% rollout" means to a real engineer
Kill switch design — what happens when something breaks at 2am
Audit log — who changed what, and why that matters legally
CONTEXT: "I'm the PM for feature management and experimentation at Azure. This is literally what I work on."
WEEK 04
Let's build an AI chat interface — responsible design from someone who ships AI
Build: Chat UI with loading states, streaming text, error handling, retry
Expectation setting — the spinner is a promise
Graceful degradation — what happens when the model is down?
Trust signals — why "AI can make mistakes" disclosures matter
Streaming UX — why character-by-character output feels alive
CONTEXT: "I build the tooling that makes AI agent rollout safe across Azure and Foundry."
WEEK 05
Let's build an onboarding flow — your first 60 seconds matter more than your product
Build: Multi-step onboarding wizard — progress indicator, skip options, value-first
Progressive onboarding — earn trust before asking for data
Cognitive load — one question per screen. Always.
Value-first design — show the product before asking for an account
Drop-off points — where people leave and why
CONTEXT: "Developer tools live or die at first run. I've watched activation data on products used by millions."
WEEK 06
Let's design error states — the most neglected screen in every product
Build: Error state component library — 404, 500, empty state, permission denied
Empathy in copy — "Something went wrong" is not an error message
Actionable recovery — every error needs a next step
Tone calibration — a 404 can be funny. A data loss can't.
CONTEXT: "The error state is where users decide whether to trust you or leave."
WEEK 07
Let's build a settings page — accessibility is a product decision, not a checklist
Build: Settings UI with keyboard navigation, focus states, colour contrast pass
WCAG 2.1 AA — what it means in practice vs. in spec
Keyboard-first design — tab order as product logic
Colour contrast — why your grey text is unreadable to 1 in 12 users
Focus states — the most deleted thing in every CSS reset
CONTEXT: "When 80 million people use your product, roughly 12 million have some form of disability."
WEEK 08
Let's build a deployment view — observability as UX
Build: Live deployment status panel — health, rollout %, recent events, rollback button
Signal vs. noise — what to surface vs. what to filter
Alerting fatigue — when everything is red, red means nothing
Actionable metrics — a number without a next action is just anxiety
The rollback button — why it needs to be fast
CONTEXT: "The difference between a good deploy dashboard and a bad one is whether your on-call engineer panics at 2am."

// the whole palette
// artist
Born in the vibrant yet excruciating chaos of Dhaka, Nuzhat Minhaz is a Bangladeshi artist whose work explores the shapeshifting and conflicting nature of identity through charcoal, graphite, and the occasional defiant splash of red. She draws figures who are neither fully here nor fully there — women caught between belonging and longing, strength and grief, visibility and erasure.
Art teacher at 17. National athlete at 16. Cybersecurity startup founder at 19. Tech PM at 22. Solo exhibit by the Seattle waterfront at 26. She is not one thing. That has always been the point.
"I have always drawn what I couldn't say. The figures in my work are not portraits of absence — they are portraits of the weight of being present in a world that keeps demanding you translate yourself."
Her mediums — charcoal, graphite 6B and 8B, ink and brush, acrylic — are chosen for what they resist. Charcoal smears. Graphite ghosts. Ink doesn't forgive. Acrylic covers what came before, eventually.
// মেঘের পরে মেঘ — clouds upon clouds
BIDESHINI — show statement
Solo exhibition · Seattle waterfront · 2026
I have been called Bideshini — a woman from a foreign land. Unreachable because my thoughts can never fully translate, and even when they do, the translation carries something that isn't quite the feeling anymore.
"This show is my passport of contradictions. It is the archive of what I carried, what I couldn't leave behind, and what I became in the distance between."
These 16 works were made across three countries and a decade of displacement. Not all displacement is painful. Some of it is chosen. Some of it is the price you pay for becoming who you needed to be.
The figures in these works are not me. They are all of me. The girl who got on a plane alone. The woman who won who knew something was being lost in the winning.
মেঘের পরে মেঘ — Clouds upon clouds. "Clouds upon clouds gather in the sky." A Tagore line about accumulation, about what builds before the break.
